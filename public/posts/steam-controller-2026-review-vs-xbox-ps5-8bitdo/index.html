A Lazy July Afternoon with a New Controller#

It’s mid-July 2026. The fan on my desk is humming softly, there’s a glass of iced something on the coaster, and I’ve got a brand-new controller in my hands. Not just any controller — Valve’s 2026 Steam Controller, the one they dropped back in May and I’ve been meaning to sit down with properly ever since.

There’s something about summer that makes you want to slow down and just play. No spreadsheets, no benchmarking, no “is this worth it” analysis. Just a warm room, a cold drink, and a controller that feels good in your hands. That’s the spirit I’m approaching this in.

Valve’s second attempt at a Steam Controller is here. The first one came out in 2015, and let’s be honest — it was a divisive piece of hardware. So when they announced a new version in 2026, I had to know: did they actually listen to the criticism, or is this the same idea with a fresh coat of paint?

Grab a drink. Let’s talk about it.

The Backstory: Valve’s Second Attempt#

If you’ve been around PC gaming for a while, you remember the 2015 Steam Controller. It was bold, ambitious, and deeply polarizing. That dual-trackpad design was unlike anything else on the market, and the shape — well, opinions on the shape ran the full spectrum from “genius” to “what were they thinking.”

The trackpads were clever for what they could do. Mouse emulation on a controller? That was a first. But for a lot of people, it was confusing. The learning curve was steep, and the unconventional button layout meant you couldn’t just pick it up and play like you could with any Xbox or PlayStation controller.

Valve heard all of that. And this time around, they came back with something that feels like a response to every piece of feedback they got.

The 2026 Steam Controller, released on May 4th, keeps the trackpad — because let’s face it, that’s the one feature that actually made people sit up and pay attention — but wraps it in a much more conventional button layout. The ergonomics are noticeably improved. It still has that Valve personality, but it doesn’t fight you for the first twenty minutes.

At €99 (roughly $109 USD), it’s not exactly budget-friendly. But it’s also not trying to be the cheapest option on the shelf. It’s trying to be the best option for one very specific audience: people who live in Steam.

And honestly? I think they’re onto something.

First Impressions#

I unboxed it on a Tuesday evening. The packaging is clean, understated — very Valve. No flashy marketing speak, no “NEXT-GEN” banners. Just the controller and a USB-C cable.

The first thing I noticed was the weight. It’s substantial without being heavy. Not the featherlight plastic feel of some budget controllers, but not dense enough to cause fatigue during a long session. It sits in your hands in a way that just… works. The grip contours feel considered, and the buttons are placed where your fingers naturally fall.

The trackpad is still there, of course, taking up that central space between the grips. It’s smooth, responsive, and feels like it belongs. Unlike the 2015 version, where the trackpads felt like the entire personality of the controller, this one integrates them as one tool among many.

For €99, the materials feel premium. The matte finish resists fingerprints reasonably well, the buttons have a satisfying click, and the triggers don’t have that mushy, over-travel feel that plagues some controllers in this price range.

Is it the most beautiful controller I’ve ever held? No. But it doesn’t need to be. It’s a tool, and it’s a well-made one. It pairs nicely with the desktop I built from spare parts — this whole setup feels made for long, relaxed gaming sessions.

Steam Controller vs. Xbox Wireless Controller#

Let’s start with the obvious comparison. The Xbox Wireless Controller is the default. It’s the controller that every PC gamer knows, that every game supports out of the box, and that you can buy at pretty much any store on the planet for around $60.

The Xbox controller is the reliable workhorse. It’s familiar, comfortable, and universally compatible. You plug it in or pair it via Bluetooth, and it just works. There’s nothing particularly exciting about it, and that’s exactly the point.

Where the Steam Controller pulls ahead:

The Steam Controller’s superpower is Steam Input. That’s Valve’s controller configuration system, and it’s deep. Per-game profiles, custom button mappings, gyro aiming, trackpad mouse emulation — it’s all there. If you’re playing exclusively on Steam, this controller unlocks features that the Xbox controller simply can’t match.

The trackpad is the big differentiator. For strategy games, menu navigation, and even FPS aim assist in casual modes, having a precision pointing surface built into your controller is genuinely useful.

The real question: Do you want universal compatibility, or do you want Steam-native features?

If you play across multiple platforms, use emulators, or jump between Epic, GOG, and Steam regularly, the Xbox controller is the safer bet. If you live in Steam — Big Picture mode, Remote Play, the whole ecosystem — the Steam Controller gives you a richer experience.

Steam Controller vs. DualSense (PS5)#

The DualSense is Sony’s answer to “what if a controller could feel more alive?” And honestly, it delivers. The adaptive triggers — where the L2 and R2 buttons vary their resistance depending on what you’re doing in-game — are genuinely impressive. Pulling a taut bowstring feels different from pressing a light trigger. The haptic feedback system, using dual actuators instead of traditional rumble motors, gives you nuanced vibrations you can actually distinguish from each other.

At around $70, the DualSense is also cheaper than the Steam Controller. It works on PS5, PC, Mac, and even mobile. USB-C rechargeable, plenty of color options, and it looks great.

Where the Steam Controller holds its own:

The DualSense is a fantastic controller, but it’s fundamentally a PlayStation controller that happens to work on PC. The Steam Controller is built for PC from the ground up. No Sony lock-in, no platform restrictions. The trackpad gives you functionality the DualSense simply doesn’t have, and the Steam Input integration means you get per-game customization more granular than what DualSense offers on PC.

The real question: Do you want cutting-edge haptic feedback technology, or do you want a controller that’s native to the PC ecosystem?

If you play on PS5 and PC, the DualSense is the obvious choice. One controller for both platforms, with features that genuinely elevate the experience. If you’re PC-only and you live in Steam, the Steam Controller’s ecosystem advantages start to outweigh the DualSense’s hardware advantages.

Steam Controller vs. 8BitDo#

8BitDo has carved out a really nice niche. Their controllers — the Ultimate series, the Pro 2, the Pro 3, the Arcade Controller Pro — range from about $50 to $70 and offer a lot of value. Retro aesthetics, multi-platform support (Switch, PC, mobile, Android), USB-C charging, and in some cases, compact designs that are great for travel.

8BitDo controllers feel like they were made by people who love retro gaming and want to bring that experience to modern platforms. They’re charming, they’re affordable, and they work across pretty much everything.

Where the Steam Controller differs:

This isn’t really a fair comparison, because these controllers are targeting different audiences. 8BitDo is about retro charm, compact size, and multi-platform versatility at a great price. The Steam Controller is about deep integration with one specific platform — Steam — and offering features like the trackpad and Steam Input profiles that no other controller in this price range provides.

If you’re a retro gamer, a Switch player, or someone who wants a compact controller for travel, 8BitDo is your brand. If you’re a Steam-centric PC gamer who wants the most feature-rich controller experience on that platform, the Steam Controller is worth the premium.

Neither is “better.” They’re just different.

The Bigger Picture: Controllers in a Shortage Era#

We can’t talk about buying any consumer electronics right now without mentioning the elephant in the room: the memory chip shortage.

Between 2025 and 2026, the AI boom has created unprecedented demand for RAM and memory chips from data centers. That supply chain pressure has rippled out into consumer electronics, and controllers are no exception. Stock has been spotty across the board — not just for the Steam Controller, but for Xbox, DualSense, and 8BitDo too.

The AI data center boom is reshaping hardware availability. The same memory chips that power large language models are the ones that end up in your gaming peripherals. When demand from data centers outpaces production, consumer electronics feel the squeeze. For now, that means longer wait times, occasional stock-outs, and prices that don’t always reflect the sticker price.

Valve’s own production pace hasn’t helped. The Steam Controller isn’t a mass-market product in the same way the Xbox controller is, so supply has been limited from day one. If you’re interested in one, you may need to be patient. Check the Steam store periodically, and don’t be surprised if you have to wait a few weeks.

This shortage context is also worth keeping in mind when evaluating that €99 price tag. Part of what you’re paying for is the fact that these things aren’t sitting on every store shelf.

Hades II#

Supergiant Games’ roguelike sequel is in Early Access, and it’s already excellent. The controller support is tight — every dodge, attack, and dash feels responsive. Roguelikes are perfect for controller play because they’re built around short, intense bursts of action. You can squeeze in a run between chores, or lose three hours without noticing. The Steam Controller’s trackpad makes menu navigation between runs quick and precise, which matters when you’re cycling through boons and upgrades.

Stardew Valley#

The ultimate cozy game. Full controller compatibility, a relaxed pace, and zero pressure. Stardew Valley is what you play on those lazy summer afternoons when you just want to tend your virtual farm, fish in the river, and maybe visit the beach. There’s nothing to rush. The Steam Controller’s comfortable ergonomics make it easy to play for hours without your hands getting tired.

Counter-Strike 2#

The definitive Steam FPS. Free-to-play now, endlessly competitive, and it just keeps getting updates. While most competitive CS players are on keyboard and mouse, the Steam Controller’s trackpad actually holds its own in casual modes. The mouse emulation gives you precision that standard analog aiming can’t match, and the per-game profiles let you dial in exactly the sensitivity you want. It’s not going to replace your mouse for ranked play, but for relaxed weekend sessions, it’s surprisingly capable.

Time Crisis 4#

Pure arcade nostalgia. Time Crisis was built for light-gun controllers, but the Steam Controller’s trackpad gives you something close to that pointing experience. Fast-paced action, cover-based shooting, and that retro arcade energy that screams summer gaming sessions with friends. The trackpad shines here in a way it doesn’t in many other games — it feels like the tool it was designed to be.

Euro Truck Simulator 2#

The ultimate relaxing simulator. Controller-friendly, meditative gameplay, and hauling cargo across Europe at sunset is oddly therapeutic. There’s a reason this game has such a devoted following. You hop in the cab, pick a route, put on some music, and just drive. The Steam Controller’s comfortable grip makes long sessions easy, and the game is frequently available for just €5 on Steam sales. If you’re looking for a summer game that helps you unwind, this is it.

Pros and Cons#

After spending a solid chunk of July with the Steam Controller, here’s my honest take.

What Works Well#

  • Deep Steam integration — Steam Input, per-game profiles, Big Picture mode, and Remote Play all work seamlessly. If you live in Steam, this controller feels native.
  • The trackpad — Still the standout feature in this space. Mouse emulation for strategy games and FPS aim assist is a real quality-of-life upgrade.
  • Build quality — For €99, the materials and finish feel premium. No creaky buttons, no mushy triggers.
  • A genuine second attempt — Valve listened to criticism of the 2015 version. Better ergonomics, more conventional buttons, and the Steam ecosystem is years deeper.

Where It Falls Short#

  • Steam-centric — Outside of Steam, this controller loses its special sauce. No unique features in Epic, GOG, or other platforms.
  • €99 is not cheap — In a market where the Xbox controller is ~$60 and 8BitDo offers great value, the price premium demands justification.
  • The trackpad is polarizing — If you’ve never used a trackpad on a controller, it takes adjustment. Some hands simply don’t work with it.
  • Availability — Between the RAM chip shortage squeezing consumer electronics and Valve’s own production pace, stock can be spotty. You may need to wait.

Final Thoughts#

Valve’s second attempt at a Steam Controller is, I think, worth giving a try. It’s a genuine improvement over the 2015 original, and it feels like a product built by people who actually read the reviews and took notes.

The €99 price tag is steep, especially in a market where great controllers exist for half that cost. But if you’re a Steam-centric gamer — if you play in Big Picture mode, use Remote Play, and live in the Steam ecosystem — the integration advantages are real. The trackpad remains the one feature no other controller in this space offers, and it pays off in the right games.

The timing hasn’t been ideal. The memory chip shortage means stock is spotty and prices are firm, but that’s a broader market issue that affects every controller on this list, not just Valve’s.

So, back to where we started: it’s a warm July afternoon, the fan is humming, there’s an iced drink on the desk, and I’m playing something that doesn’t require me to think too hard. The Steam Controller sits in my hands, comfortable and unobtrusive, and for this particular setup — Steam, PC, summer couch — it does exactly what it’s supposed to do.
